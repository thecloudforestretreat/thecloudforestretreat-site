Pululahua

Pululahua is an ancient volcanic caldera where cloud and sunlight move fast, farms sit inside a crater, and the landscape tells a story that is thousands of years old.

A volcano you can walk inside

Geology, caldera life, and fertile soils

Pululahua is best known for its enormous caldera, a crater formed after volcanic activity and collapse. The fertile soils inside the crater support small farming communities, making it one of the rare inhabited calderas on Earth.

The name “Pululahua” is commonly linked to indigenous Kichwa meanings associated with mist and water, which fits the frequent fog and cloud that drift through the reserve.

Type: volcanic caldera Feel: misty viewpoints Soils: naturally fertile
Best moment

Go when the clouds are moving. You will watch the crater appear and disappear, like the landscape is breathing.

What you can do there

Easy to visit, hard to forget

Pululahua is famous for scenic overlooks (miradores), short hikes, and quiet exploration through changing microclimates. The reserve also supports diverse plant life, including orchids and bromeliads, and offers excellent birdwatching opportunities.

  • Mirador views: panoramic crater vistas and patchwork farmland
  • Hiking: trails down into the caldera and around the rim
  • Birdwatching: hummingbirds and cloud forest species
  • Photography: dramatic skies, mist, and layered ridgelines
Vibe: quiet and scenic Best for: photos and walks Tip: bring a light jacket
Respect the reserve

Stay on trails, avoid loud music, and pack out what you bring in. Misty ecosystems are delicate and recover slowly.
